Cost Optimization: What I Am Building

By Bryce Murad · April 24, 2026

I've been running this.live for over two years now, and the biggest lesson I've learned isn't about product-market fit or user acquisition. It's about cost optimization. When you're building a live streaming platform that processes millions of video streams, every dollar matters. More importantly, every architecture decision compounds into either sustainable growth or unsustainable burn.

The cloud providers want you to believe that auto-scaling and managed services solve everything. They don't. What they do is create a convenient abstraction that hides where your money goes until your AWS bill hits five figures and you realize you're paying $0.50 per GB for CloudFront data transfer when you could be paying $0.02 with a proper CDN strategy.

Let me walk you through the real costs of running a live streaming platform and how we've optimized each layer.

Video processing is where most companies hemorrhage money. AWS Elemental MediaLive charges $0.0075 per minute for a single-pipeline HD stream. That sounds reasonable until you're processing 10,000 concurrent streams and your monthly bill is $3.24 million just for transcoding. We moved our video processing to dedicated GPU instances running FFmpeg on NVIDIA T4 cards. A single g4dn.xlarge instance at $0.526 per hour can handle roughly 50 concurrent HD transcodes. That brings our per-stream cost down to about $0.00035 per minute, a 95% reduction.

The trick isn't just using bare metal instead of managed services. It's understanding the specific workload characteristics. Live streaming has predictable CPU patterns and burst requirements. We use spot instances for 70% of our transcoding capacity with a fallback to on-demand instances during peak hours. Spot instances for g4dn.xlarge average around $0.16 per hour, cutting costs by another 70%.

Database costs tell a similar story. RDS Aurora Serverless seemed perfect for our variable workloads until we hit scale. Aurora charges $0.50 per ACU-hour, and each ACU provides 2GB of memory. For our real-time chat and stream metadata, we were burning through 200 ACUs during peak traffic. That's $7,200 monthly just for database compute, not including storage and I/O.

We migrated to self-managed PostgreSQL on r5.2xlarge instances with pgBouncer connection pooling and read replicas. The monthly cost dropped to $1,400 for equivalent performance. The maintenance overhead is real, but when you're saving $70,000 annually, it's worth hiring someone who knows database administration.

Content delivery is where the biggest savings hide. CloudFront's pricing starts at $0.085 per GB for the first 10TB monthly. For a video platform, that's maybe two weeks of traffic. We're now using a multi-CDN approach with Bunny CDN at $0.01 per GB and Cloudflare's enterprise pricing at roughly $0.02 per GB. The savings compound quickly when you're pushing 500TB monthly.

The architecture change required rebuilding our edge logic. Instead of relying on CloudFront's Lambda@Edge, we implemented edge routing with Workers and Bunny's edge scripting. The performance is actually better because we can implement smarter geographic routing based on real latency measurements, not just geographic proximity.

Storage costs seem small until they're not. S3 Standard storage at $0.023 per GB seems reasonable for video archives until you realize that 90% of your content is never accessed after 30 days. We implemented an automated lifecycle that moves streams to S3 Intelligent-Tiering after 24 hours and Glacier Flexible Retrieval after 90 days. Archive storage costs dropped from $15,000 monthly to $3,200.

The real optimization came from changing our recording strategy entirely. Instead of storing full-quality archives, we generate multiple quality tiers and only keep the highest quality for streams that meet specific engagement thresholds. Most streams get archived at 720p, which cuts storage requirements by 60% with negligible user impact.

Network costs are the hidden killer in real-time applications. Inter-AZ data transfer at $0.01 per GB doesn't sound significant until you're replicating terabytes of live stream data for redundancy. We restructured our infrastructure to minimize cross-AZ traffic by implementing regional stream processing clusters. Each availability zone handles its own transcoding and only sends metadata across zones.

WebRTC signaling creates another network cost that nobody talks about. Socket.io connections on Application Load Balancers cost $0.0225 per hour per connection. With 50,000 concurrent viewers, that's $1,125 daily just for load balancer connections. We moved signaling to dedicated instances running behind Network Load Balancers, cutting that cost by 80%.

Monitoring and observability can quietly drain budgets if you're not careful. CloudWatch costs $0.50 per million API requests and $0.30 per GB ingested for logs. Our real-time analytics were generating 2 billion API requests monthly. We moved primary metrics collection to Prometheus running on dedicated instances and only send critical alerts to CloudWatch. The monthly savings: $8,400.

The biggest mindset shift was moving from "cloud-native" to "cloud-smart." Managed services have their place, especially for non-core functionality, but blind adoption leads to architectural choices that optimize for development velocity at the expense of unit economics.

Infrastructure cost optimization isn't just about switching providers or negotiating better rates. It requires understanding your actual usage patterns, identifying the constraints that matter for your specific workload, and building architecture that aligns technical decisions with business metrics. Every technical choice has a cost structure, and those costs compound as you scale.

The real lesson from two years of optimizing this.live is that cost optimization isn't a one-time project. It's an ongoing discipline that requires measuring everything, questioning every managed service, and always asking whether you're paying for convenience or capability.
