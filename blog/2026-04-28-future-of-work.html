Future Of Work: What I Am Building

By Bryce Murad · April 28, 2026

The future of work isn't some distant sci-fi scenario. It's happening right now in my terminal, and the changes are more radical than most people realize. Over the past few months, building Hermes Agent has given me a front-row seat to how AI is fundamentally reshaping not just what we do, but how we think about productivity itself.

I've been deep in the weeds fixing OAuth token chains for Azure Anthropic endpoints and debugging API version parameters that were malforming URLs. These aren't glamorous tasks, but they're the kind of technical plumbing that reveals something profound about where we're headed. The integration complexity between different AI providers like OpenAI, Anthropic, and Azure's variants tells a story about the new infrastructure layer emerging beneath knowledge work.

What strikes me most is how the traditional boundaries between human and machine capabilities are dissolving in unexpected ways. When I'm troubleshooting why the skills search RPC broke in our TUI, I'm not just writing code anymore. I'm orchestrating a conversation between multiple AI systems, each with different strengths and API quirks. The Azure OpenAI GPT-5.x models handle certain reasoning tasks differently than Claude, and Azure Anthropic has its own authentication dance that bypasses the standard OAuth flow entirely.

This isn't automation in the factory sense. It's augmentation at the cognitive level, and it requires a completely different mental model. The future worker doesn't just use AI tools. They architect AI workflows, debug multi-provider failures, and design human-AI collaboration patterns that didn't exist six months ago.

The cost dynamics alone are reshaping everything. Running Claude Sonnet through Azure costs about $15 per million tokens for input and $75 per million for output. GPT-4o is roughly $5 input and $15 output. These aren't just pricing differences. They're forcing us to think architecturally about which models handle which parts of a workflow. I find myself designing systems where cheaper models do the heavy lifting and expensive ones handle the critical reasoning, all orchestrated through custom endpoints that preserve query parameters correctly.

The technical complexity is real, but it's creating new categories of work rather than eliminating existing ones. Someone needs to understand why Azure Anthropic requires bypassing the OAuth token refresh to prevent credentials.json from overwriting Azure keys mid-session. Someone needs to architect the provider detection logic that auto-detects transport protocols and context lengths. These are new skills for a new kind of infrastructure.

What's happening reminds me of the early cloud computing transition, except compressed into months instead of years. Companies are realizing they need people who can navigate the maze of AI providers, understand the nuances of different model architectures, and design systems that gracefully handle the inevitable failures. When your production workflow depends on API calls to five different AI services, traditional DevOps isn't enough. You need people who understand both the technical plumbing and the cognitive characteristics of each model.

The talent implications are staggering. I'm seeing engineers pivot from pure software development to becoming AI workflow architects. They're not being replaced by AI. They're becoming the conductors of AI orchestras, and the music they're making is unlike anything we've heard before. The demand for people who can build reliable AI integration layers is exploding, and the supply is almost nonexistent.

But here's what really keeps me up at night: the pace of change is accelerating. The Hermes Agent codebase I'm working on today will look primitive in six months. New models are launching weekly, each with different strengths, costs, and integration requirements. The half-life of AI knowledge is shrinking rapidly, which means the future of work isn't just about adapting to AI. It's about building systems and careers that can adapt continuously.

The organizations that win won't be the ones with the best AI tools. They'll be the ones with the best AI integration capabilities. They'll have people who understand how to chain different models together, how to handle provider failovers gracefully, and how to design human oversight into automated workflows. They'll treat AI providers like cloud infrastructure, with multi-region deployments, cost optimization strategies, and disaster recovery plans.

I'm seeing this firsthand in how we handle Azure Foundry integration. It's not just about calling an API. It's about understanding transport layer auto-detection, managing environment variables across deployment contexts, and building provider-agnostic abstractions that can adapt as the ecosystem evolves. These are the new core competencies.

The future worker is part developer, part systems architect, and part cognitive scientist. They understand both the technical constraints of different AI models and the human factors that make AI-human collaboration effective. They can debug a malformed URL in an Azure OpenAI call and design a conversation flow that leverages Claude's reasoning strengths. Most importantly, they can do all of this while the ground shifts beneath their feet every few months.

This isn't about humans versus machines. It's about humans orchestrating machines in increasingly sophisticated ways. The complexity is real, but so is the opportunity. We're not just automating existing work. We're inventing entirely new categories of valuable human activity.

The transition won't be smooth, and not everyone will make it. But for those who can navigate the technical complexity and embrace the continuous learning required, the future of work looks less like displacement and more like expansion into cognitive territories we're only beginning to explore.
