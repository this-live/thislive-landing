The receipt convention keeps the work honest

June 22, 2026

There is a difference between what a system does and what a system tells you it does. Before I built the receipt convention, those two things were almost always different. The crons fired on the Mac mini and the DGX Spark, the agents ran, the Telegram bots chattered, and I assumed everything was working. It was not. Something is always quietly wrong. The receipt convention is how I stopped lying to myself about it.

Why I needed receipts

I was building agents across five different repos, running crons at different intervals on different machines, with multiple agents reading from and writing to shared state. Most of the time the system appeared functional. When it was not, the failure looked the same as the success from the outside. A cron fired. A model responded. A database received a write. The output was the same whether the write was correct or not. That is the worst kind of problem, because you do not know it exists until something breaks in public.

The turning point was a cron that had been firing silently for weeks, producing output that looked correct, but was actually writing to the wrong database. Nobody noticed because the output format matched what we expected. I would have noticed faster if every cron action had written a short note about what it did and what it expected to happen next. The note would have shown that the write went to the wrong place. The note would have flagged it within hours, not weeks.

What a receipt is

A receipt is a short note written every time an agent does something consequential. Consequential means it changes state, makes a decision, or triggers the next step in a chain. A receipt contains three things: what happened, what was observed, and what is expected next. That is it. No prose. No narrative. Three fields. A timestamp. A scope tag. Cortex writes receipts when it deploys changes. MNEMOS writes receipts when it indexes new memories. The agents that matter are the ones that write receipts, because the receipts are the proof that they did the thing.

The scope tag is the important part. A customer-support bot does not write receipts that include personal scheduling data. A deployment script does not write receipts that include model output. Each part of the system writes receipts only about its own scope. The scoping is what makes the system honest. If a support bot could write about everything, the receipts would be noise, and noise hides the signal.

The receipts go into a folder on the machine. That is all. There is no database for receipts. There is no index. There is a folder and a daily job that reads the folder. The daily job writes me a one-page summary. I read the summary with coffee. If a receipt is missing for something that should have written one, the daily job flags it. That is the whole system.

The daily path

The things I do every morning should be the things that are easiest to do. The daily path is: a cron fires at 6am, an agent reads the receipt folder from the previous day, writes a one-page summary, drops it into Telegram, and the day starts. I read the summary. I see what ran, what did not run, what looked wrong, what needs attention. If everything on the summary is fine, I close Telegram and get on with the day. If something is wrong, the summary tells me what and where, and I investigate.

This is not a dashboard. A dashboard asks me to look for problems. The receipt summary pushes problems at me. There is a difference. A dashboard is a place I visit when I feel like investigating. A summary is something I receive whether I feel like it or not. The daily path is boring on purpose. Boring means it works. Boring means I do not have to think about it. The things that are boring are the things I want to stop thinking about.

What the receipts exposed

After running the receipt system for a month, the picture of what is actually happening across the fleet is very different from the picture I had before. The receipts show me which crons are firing uselessly, which agents are spinning their wheels, which models are producing correct output that nobody reads, which services are talking to each other but nobody designed that conversation. The receipts do not lie. They are not elegant. They are not exciting. They are the closest thing I have to a honest accounting of the work.

The most useful thing the receipts revealed is what I call ghost activity. A service runs every five minutes. It produces output. It writes to a database. The output looks fine. Nobody is reading it. Nobody needs it. The service exists because I built it three months ago and never had the chance to remove it. The receipts flag ghost activity because there is no downstream consumer writing the next receipt in the chain. The chain stops. The chain stops means something is running in isolation. Isolation is fine for a few days while you figure it out. Isolation for three months means you should kill the cron.

Tradeoffs I have accepted

The receipt system is not perfect. It writes more notes than I sometimes want to read. The daily summary is sometimes too long because too many services are running. When that happens, I do not improve the filtering. I kill the services. The receipt system is a signal. If the signal is too noisy, the problem is not the signal. The problem is the noise. The fix is to remove the noise, not to filter it better. That is the discipline the receipts enforce.

I have also learned that not every agent action deserves a receipt. Routine checks that confirm nothing has changed do not need a note. Receipts are for the things that change state or make decisions. If nothing changed, the system should not say anything. The silence is the receipt. Or rather, the absence of a receipt is the receipt. That distinction matters. You do not need a paper trail for every step. You need a paper trail for the decisions.

What I will say in three months

If you ask me in three months how the receipt system is different, I will tell you what the operational debt looks like then. I will tell you which crons I have killed because the receipts proved they were useless. I will tell you which services I rebuilt because the receipts showed a missing link in the chain. I will tell you what the daily summary looks like when it is down to a page that is actually useful. The honest update is the one I owe you. The honest update is what the receipts produce.

Thanks for reading. If you are building something similar, I would love to hear what is working for you. The systems I have are the systems I needed. Yours will be different. That is the point.
