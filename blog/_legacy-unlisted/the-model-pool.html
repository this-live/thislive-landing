The Model Pool: Why I Use Fifteen Models Instead of One

By Bryce Murad · this.live

I do not use one AI model. I use a fleet of them, and understanding why requires letting go of a very natural human tendency: the desire to find the one best answer.

We want to know which model is best. Is it GPT-4o? Is it Claude? Is it DeepSeek? We want a winner. We want to crown a champion and be done with it.

That mindset is outdated. It made sense when there were two or three models to choose from. It makes no sense when there are twenty, each with different strengths, weaknesses, costs, and personalities.

Here is what I have plugged into Maestro right now. OpenAI's GPT-4o and o3-mini. Anthropic's Claude Sonnet and Haiku. Google's Gemini. xAI's Grok. DeepSeek's V3 and R1. Perplexity for search-augmented answers. Qwen Coder for programming tasks. Moonshot's Kimi K2.6 for long-context reasoning. MiniMax for fast conversational responses. And on my own hardware, I run local models through vLLM and Ollama, including Gemma and distilled variants.

Each of these has a different personality, different strengths, different cost structure, and different context window size. Claude is cautious and thorough. It will flag edge cases you did not ask about. GPT-4o is versatile. It can switch between coding, writing, and analysis without missing a beat. DeepSeek is cheap and surprisingly capable, especially for reasoning tasks. Kimi handles enormous context windows without losing track of details. Qwen Coder is my go-to for actual programming. MiniMax is fast and great for back-and-forth conversation. Local models are essentially free once you own the hardware, and they keep your data private.

The idea that you should pick one and stick with it is like saying you should only own one tool in your workshop. A hammer is great for nails. It is terrible for screws. A screwdriver is great for screws. It is terrible for cutting wood. You need a toolbox.

The future is multi-model. You route to the right model for the task. You use consensus to verify critical outputs. You fall back to cheap models for simple tasks and bring out the heavy hitters when it matters. You run local models for sensitive data and cloud models for cutting-edge capability.

Maestro makes this invisible. You talk to your agent. Maestro handles the rest. But under the hood, it is a symphony of different intelligences working together, each playing the part it is best at.

That is the architecture that scales. That is the architecture that saves money. That is the architecture that does not depend on any single vendor's roadmap or pricing.

Stop looking for the best model. Start building the best ensemble.
