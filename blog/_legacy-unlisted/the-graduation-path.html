The Graduation Path: Building Software That Lets You Leave

By Bryce Murad · this.live

Maestro was built with a graduation path in mind. I think this is one of the most important things about it, and almost no one else in this space does this. Most SaaS companies want to trap you. The deeper you go, the harder it is to leave. I built Maestro the exact opposite way.

Here is the idea. When you start, you want everything to just work. You do not want to think about API keys, model selection, cost optimization, or security. You want an agent that answers your messages and helps you get things done. Maestro's Starter plan gives you that. One subscription, one setup, done.

But as you grow, you develop opinions. You want to bring your own API keys. You want to use specific models. You want to customize routing. You want to stop paying us for usage entirely and just use Maestro as the orchestration layer.

So we built a graduation path. At any point, you can bring your own keys. We pass zero percent of your API costs through to you. You keep the routing, the consensus, the security, the connectors. You just pay for the infrastructure, not the tokens.

Eventually, you might want to self-host entirely. Run Maestro on your own hardware or cloud account. We support that too. The same Docker container, the same codebase, running wherever you want. You own it. We just built it.

Most SaaS companies trap you with data lock-in, custom formats, and proprietary integrations. The deeper you go, the harder it is to leave. I think that is ethically wrong and practically stupid. If your product is good, people stay because they want to. Not because they cannot figure out how to leave.

That is how you build trust. You make it easy to leave. You make the graduation path obvious and supported. You celebrate when a customer outgrows your managed tier because it means your product enabled them to become independent.

I believe in democratizing access to AI. That does not mean locking people into my platform. It means giving them the tools to succeed, whether they stay with me or not.

If you are building a product, ask yourself: does your business model depend on trapping customers? Or does it depend on creating so much value that they want to stay?

I know which one I chose.
