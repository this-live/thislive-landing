Quorum: Why I Run Three Models for Every Important Decision

By Bryce Murad · this.live

Here is a dirty secret about large language models: they hallucinate. Not often, but often enough that you cannot trust a single model with high-stakes decisions. A single LLM is a pattern matcher with no ground truth. It predicts what words should come next based on training data. Most of the time it is right. Sometimes it is confidently wrong. The problem is you cannot tell the difference just by looking at the output.

I learned this the hard way. Early on, I had an agent review a security configuration. It looked fine. I deployed it. Turns out the agent had missed a critical exposure because it was pattern-matching against similar configs it had seen rather than actually analyzing the specific rules in front of it. It cost me a day of cleanup and a lot of stress.

That is when I built Quorum.

Quorum is Maestro's consensus system. For high-stakes tasks, the same request gets sent to multiple models simultaneously. Not sequentially. In parallel. Each model generates its own response independently, without knowing what the others are saying. Then a consensus agent compares the outputs, identifies where they agree, flags where they disagree, and synthesizes a final answer that represents the strongest consensus.

This is not just about accuracy. It is about confidence. When three different models, trained by different organizations on different data, all arrive at the same conclusion, you can trust that conclusion. When they disagree, the consensus agent surfaces the disagreement so you can investigate.

The models in my consensus pool are intentionally diverse. Claude tends to be cautious and thorough. GPT-4o is more of a generalist. Kimi handles enormous context without losing details. DeepSeek reasons differently because of its training architecture. When they agree, you have overcome each model's individual biases and blind spots.

I use Quorum for code reviews, security analysis, architectural decisions, and anything where a mistake would be expensive. It costs more per request because you are running multiple models, but it eliminates the need to double-check everything yourself. I would rather pay for three opinions on a security review than pay for one breach.

There is a deeper point here that I think a lot of people miss. The future of reliable AI is not about finding the one best model. It is about orchestrating many models, each with different strengths, and combining their outputs intelligently. The ensemble approach has been standard in machine learning for decades. It is time we applied it to LLMs.

Quorum is my implementation of that idea. A single brain can make mistakes. A panel of experts agreeing on something is as close to truth as you are going to get.

And in a world where AI is making more decisions that matter, truth is worth paying for.
