The blog archive needed a truth pass

By Bryce Murad · June 08, 2026

Cleaning up the public record is part of building in public

The archive had started lying by accident

I do not mean that dramatically. I mean it literally. Some old posts said I had been running This.Live for a year or two. That is false. The real timeline is simpler: I got laid off in January, started building seriously then, and the public This.Live surface launched in March.

That kind of mistake is easy to create when agents generate content faster than the archive gets reviewed. A phrase slips in. A date drifts. A confident claim about revenue, scale, or usage shows up because it sounds plausible. Then the website starts telling a cleaner story than reality. I do not want that.

The fix is not just editing

The fix is a different standard. Dates need to make sense. Claims need to survive verification. Posts need to read like a person wrote them, not like a content calendar got lonely. That means headers, real sections, shorter paragraphs, fewer slogans, and more specificity about what is actually built versus what is still in progress.

I want the blog to feel like weekly notes from the workbench: professional enough to trust, human enough to read, and honest enough that I am not embarrassed by it later.

What changed

I consolidated the public archive into a weekly cadence from January through June. I removed or redirected duplicate and unsafe posts. I stripped false tenure claims, fake operating numbers, unsupported revenue language, and anything that made This.Live sound more mature than it is. The company is young. That is fine. The work is still real.

The cleaned archive should now do what it was supposed to do in the first place: explain the build, show the thinking, and leave a public trail without pretending the future already happened.
