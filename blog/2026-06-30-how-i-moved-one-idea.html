How I moved one idea through every surface I own

June 30, 2026

One idea, six surfaces, seven commits.

Last week I wrote about the agnostic cheat code. The substrate. The local memory that survives every model change, every surface change, every dispatch change. The post settled in and the next question surfaced, the one I had been avoiding. If the substrate is the thing that matters, how does that show up in every place someone encounters my work?

I am used to building. I am not used to being honest about the consistency part.

Because consistency is the part nobody asks about. You ask what a system does. You ask what it ships. You do not ask what it sounds like when you show up in seven different places across three days, and whether those seven appearances are actually the same thing, or just the same products rearranged into a better order.

Over the course of a week, one idea cascaded through everything I own. The philosophy page. The landing copy. The pillar cards. The animations. The resume. Six commits. Seven changes. One question running through all of them: what does it mean to say that decentralized agentic AI is the thing, and what does it mean to show that consistently across every surface?

Starting with the philosophy page.

The philosophy page had been sitting there for a while, holding the old framing. One operator, many projects. Each product a thing I built, each project a chapter. It was a portfolio. It was honest, but it was the wrong kind of honest.

Because the wrong kind of honest is the kind that tells you what you did without telling you what you are building toward. The old philosophy page described a person with a collection. The new one describes a system with a position. That is a real change. It is not cosmetic. It changes how someone reads every product page that follows.

The commit message was simple. Family influences, reframe the existing twenty-one principles. I changed the framing from portfolio to substrate. I changed the language from individual achievement to distributed positioning. I did not add anything. I changed everything.

The landing page reframe.

Once the philosophy changed, the landing page could not stay where it was. The landing page is the first surface. If the philosophy says one thing and the landing page says another, the system has a crack in it. The crack is not huge. It is the kind of crack that only the person who wrote both versions notices. But cracks grow.

The old landing page opened with a one-operator frame. Many products, one builder. The new one opens with decentralized agentic AI as the category. Not the product name. The category. That is a harder sell, because infrastructure is harder to sell than a thing with a use case. You have to convince someone that a platform matters before you show them the platform.

The landing copy changed in three commits. First the products section. Then the founder section. Then the call to action. Each one moved from the portfolio frame to the decentralized frame. The founder section is the one that hurt the most to change, because it was the most personal. It went from listing what I have built to describing what I am building. There is a difference. The difference is not marketing. The difference is honesty about direction versus honesty about inventory.

The pillar cards got a truth pass.

The six pillar cards were the next surface to shift. Each one represents a product, and each one had been written to stand on its own. Cortex, Maestro, Surfaces, Agent Fabric, Mnemos, Forge. Six separate things. Six separate pages. That was the old framing.

The new framing made each card a piece of the same system. Not six products. Six components of a distributed architecture. Each one still does its own job. Each one still has its own story. But the story changed from standalone product to system component. The animations got more specific, the copy got tighter, and the whole set reads as one thing now. Not a portfolio. A stack.

The hardest card was Forge. It had been sitting at "in training" for a while, and the truth pass that came with the decentralized reframe changed that. "Gated, ready to train" replaced "in training" because the commit message says it plainly. No adapter is training. The old status was misleading. The new status is accurate. That one change on one page is the kind of discipline that matters, even if it is easy to miss.

The resume got the same pass.

Outside the product surfaces, there is the resume. The "current focus" line changed from listing projects to saying "getting Cortex working and public." That is the same decentralized reframe, applied to something that lives outside the website. The same principle: tell someone what the work is building toward, not just what the work is right now.

Consistency is a discipline problem.

When one person owns all the surfaces, consistency is a discipline problem. Not a tooling problem. Not a system problem. A discipline problem. You have to go to each surface and ask the same question. Does this say the same thing as the rest? If it does not, fix it. If you do not fix it, the crack grows.

The tooling makes it possible. Git makes it possible. You can change six files across six commits in three days, and the whole thing shifts. But the tooling is not the hard part. The hard part is the discipline of checking every surface, every time you change one. The hard part is not letting the surface you are working on diverge from the surfaces you are not working on, simply because you forgot to go back and fix it.

I had been building with this framing in mind. I had just not been checking every surface to make sure it matched. The receipt convention taught me that forgetting a surface means the surface diverges. The decentralized reframe was the test. Did every surface hold the same idea, or did I only change the ones I was looking at?

What I would do differently.

I would check every surface before I publish. Not after. Before. Because the way these things actually work is that you change one page, it feels done, and you move on. Then three weeks later you come back and the philosophy page and the landing page and the resume and three of the pillar cards are all slightly different versions of the same idea, and nobody noticed because they are all on the same site and they are all on the same screen and the differences are small enough to overlook.

But small differences are the ones that accumulate into noise. And noise is what the decentralized reframe was supposed to replace. Instead of one operator with many projects, you say the thing. And then you make sure every surface says the same thing. Not approximately. Not close enough. The same thing.

What I will say in three months.

When the decentralization work stabilizes, I will write a post about what changed and what did not. Which surfaces held the reframe cleanly and which ones resisted. Whether the pillar animations actually communicated the system thinking or just looked better. Whether the landing page reframe is holding up under real traffic. The honest update is the one I owe you. The honest update is what the analytics produce when the dust settles.

Three months from now the decentralized framing will either be the backbone of the entire product experience or it will be one more idea that sounded good and did not survive contact with reality. I do not know which one it will be yet. That is the point of writing the honest update later, instead of today.

Thanks for reading. If you run a product that lives across multiple surfaces, you already know that consistency is harder than any single surface. I am still learning how to make it work at scale. I will share what works and what does not.
