Philosophy

What I think, and why I build the way I build.

A reference for the things I argue and the position I am building from. Long form. Read with a coffee. The line is at the end.

The AI economy exists whether you like it or not, and it is the greatest force multiplier of our time. There is no sitting it out, the way there is no sitting out participation in the economy writ large. Whether you understand it or not, you are already in the game. The only real choice is whether you have a hand on the wheel.

If you read this and you think the answer is to wait it out, the rest of this page is not for you. If you read this and you think the answer is to participate, intentionally, with the work in your own hands, then we have a lot to talk about.

The argument

The danger was never killer robots. The danger is that a few people end up holding the keys to everything. Asymmetric advantage always ends in exploitation. That is not pessimism. It is game theory. The actors with the most leverage do not have a moral obligation to use it for the rest of us. They have an economic incentive to use it for themselves. The system, left alone, produces the concentrated outcome. To get the egalitarian outcome, the system has to be designed differently. The system has to be designed so the leverage is in the hands of the many, not the few.

Max Tegmark mapped twelve ways the age of superintelligence can end. By my read, only three are actually good for people, and the line between them and the rest is whether the power is spread out and whether the AI is actually aligned to the person using it.

The first is the decentralized substrate. A world where the AI is everywhere, on everyone's hardware, pointed at everyone's intentions. No one can monopolize it, because it is not a thing that can be monopolized. The substrate is the system underneath, the way TCP/IP is the system underneath the internet. You do not own TCP/IP. You build on it. The substrate is the abundance outcome. The substrate is the one that does not end in exploitation, because the exploitation requires concentration, and the substrate is the opposite of concentration.

The second is the aligned guardian. An AI that looks out for us without running our lives. An AI that is bound to our flourishing and not to the profit of its owners. An AI that watches over the species without becoming its keeper. This is the one the serious alignment researchers are most excited about, and it is a real possibility, but it requires a particular kind of governance, a particular kind of accountability, and a particular kind of structural constraint that the current commercial AI market is not incentivized to build.

The third is what Tegmark calls the enslaved god, except decentralized. A capability so vast that no one actor can monopolize it, that runs on a billion small pieces of hardware, that is the substrate underneath the world rather than the boss of it. This is the one I am most actively working toward, because it is the one that does not require the rest of the world to agree with me. The substrate can be built by anyone, including me, in a corner, with a small team, on real hardware, in production. The guardian requires consensus. The substrate requires only a contributor. I am a contributor.

I am not interested in the quiet endings either, the ones where we are kept safe, comfortable, and stripped of any reason to strive. A future where we lose ourselves is a loss whether it is loud or quiet. Some serious researchers are fine with the endings where AI replaces us, whether it wipes us out or politely inherits the earth as our successors. They call it evolution. I do not. Evolution is not a moral argument. I want a hand in the ending. I would rather have a hand in it than watch it happen to me.

What I am not arguing

I am not arguing that AI is bad. AI is the most useful tool I have ever used, and I have been using it for years. AI is not the threat. The threat is the concentration of the substrate in the hands of a few. The threat is the asymmetric advantage that concentration produces. The threat is the capture of the substrate by the actors who have the most to gain from the substrate not being distributed. AI is a tool. The tool can be used well or used poorly. The tool is more useful in more hands. The tool is the answer, not the threat. The threat is the hoarding.

I am not arguing that the substrate will save us without help. The substrate is necessary. The substrate is not sufficient. The substrate is a tool. The rest of the work is the work.

The other things I think

I am pragmatic. The other threats are not the AI threat, but they are real, and they are not going to wait for us to solve the AI one first. Food supply. Forever chemicals. Arid land. Microplastics. Drug safety. Wars. Cultural misunderstanding. Geopolitics. I believe humanity is racing toward extinction in many ways, but also that we have the means and the increasing knowledge to fix these things. We do not have the alignment. We do not have the time. We do not have the excuse.

I am not an accelerationist. I am not a decelerationist. I am a builder with a posture. The posture is the substrate. The substrate is the position that puts a hand on the wheel. The posture is the position that does not wait for the rest of the world to agree. The posture is the position that builds the substrate, locally, now, with the resources I have, pointed at the intentions I have, and lets the rest of the world do the same with theirs.

The people who made me

The people on this page are the reason I am the way I am. If you want to understand the position, you do not need to read the bibliography. You need to read the people who raised me.

My mother, Patricia Wu-Murad

Patricia went missing in Japan shortly after retiring. The smartest, most capable woman I have ever known. She made me who I am today. A solver. Someone with a bias for action without sulking. When a problem shows up, the answer is to move, not to feel. She is the reason I build. She is the reason I do not get stuck. She is the reason the receipts matter. She is the reason the work is the work.

She subsequently died after going missing. My immediate family spent six weeks in Japan doing search and rescue, to no avail. The not-knowing was the hardest part. The lesson is operational. Life is short. Make the most of your time. And your loved ones are not forever, so let them know they are loved while you still have them. I carry that lesson into the work every day. The work is not more important than the people. The people are the work. The work is for the people.

My father, Kirk Murad

Kirk was a master class in patience and stoicism to me growing up. He taught me that being a man means to provide and protect for your family. Not to put others down. Not to flex strength. The strength is the provision. The strength is the protection. The strength is the showing up. He taught me that treating people right and being humble are good for the soul. He taught me that the simpler your needs, the happier you are. He did not say these things as principles. He lived them as posture.

The patience is the part of Kirk that I have to work at. I have the bias for action from my mother and the patience I am still building from my father. The work is the work. The work is the patience. The work is the showing up tomorrow, and the day after, and the day after, and not being the one who has to be told. The position is the showing up. The position is the humility. The position is the simpler your needs, the happier you are.

The people I learn from

I learn from the people who did the work. The athletes taught me the practice. The philosophers taught me the frame. The founders taught me the operator discipline. The modern operators taught me how to think about the current era.

The athletes

Michael Jordan turned losing into the most useful fuel of his career. He treated practice as the work, not the game. The game was the receipt. The practice was the work. The way he held himself to a standard that nobody else could see is the standard I hold myself to. I do not have Jordan's talent. I have Jordan's posture. The posture is the part that transfers.

Stephen Curry was not supposed to be an NBA star. The scouting reports said he was too small, too thin, not athletic enough. He bet on himself. He did the work. He changed the geometry of the game. The lesson is not that he changed the game. The lesson is that he bet on himself before the world bet on him.

Muhammad Ali understood that the work you do in public is part of the work. The trash talk was a tool. The trash talk made the other fighter's doubt a reality. The lesson is the use of the talk, not the talk itself.

Demetrious Johnson is the most technically complete fighter in MMA history. He is not the most powerful. He is not the most athletic. He is the most patient. The technical excellence compounds. The patience compounds. The compounding compounds.

Tiger Woods rebuilt himself from injury after injury, process after process. The lesson is not the comeback. The lesson is that the discipline is the thing that survives the injury. The discipline is the thing that does not need the talent. The discipline is the thing you can rebuild when the talent is gone.

The philosophers

Ralph Waldo Emerson wrote "Self-Reliance" in 1841, and the essay still reads like it was written for a builder in 2026. The work is the work, and the work is yours, and the work is not the consensus. The nonconformity is not a posture. The nonconformity is the position that does not come from your own conviction is a position you will not hold. The position I am building from is the position I am willing to hold.

John Stuart Mill argued in "On Liberty" (1859) that the actions of individuals should be limited only to prevent harm to other individuals. The framework I use when I think about what AI should and should not do is the Mill framework. The AI should be limited to preventing harm to the user, and to the others affected by the user's actions. The AI should not be limited to enforcing the preferences of a third party. The AI is yours.

Søren Kierkegaard argued in "Fear and Trembling" (1843) that you cannot reason your way to the commitment. You have to commit. You have to act. The reason is not sufficient. The position is the same position I take when I argue that the substrate requires the commitment, not just the argument. The build is the leap.

Friedrich Nietzsche wrote "Beyond Good and Evil" in 1886 and argued that the morality of the comfortable is the morality of the herd, and the morality of the herd is the morality that prevents the strong from being strong. The lesson is not the will to power. The lesson is the critique. The lesson is that the morality that says "be comfortable" is the morality that says "do not build."

Immanuel Kant argued in the "Critique of Pure Reason" (1781) and the "Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals" (1785) that the fundamental principle of our moral duties is a categorical imperative, and that the universal laws prescribed by the categorical imperative presuppose autonomy. The question is not "what do I want to do." The question is "what should be done, if everyone did it." The substrate is the position that should be done if everyone did it. The substrate is the position that is universal.

John Locke argued in the "Second Treatise on Civil Government" (1689) that the rights are not granted by the state, that the rights are natural, that the rights are the substrate. The capability is not granted by the operator. The capability is the substrate. The capability is yours. The operator is the state, in this analogy. The operator is the actor that does not have the right to grant or revoke the capability.

Ray Dalio wrote "Principles" in 2017, and the operating manual I keep on the shelf is the Principles. The lesson is the radical transparency. The decisions get better when the disagreement is on the table, not under it. The truth is more useful than the comfort. The substrate is the position that requires the truth to be on the table.

Richard Werner wrote "Princes of the Yen" in 2003, and the argument is the argument that the money is not what you think the money is. The money is the credit. The money is the policy. The money is the position. The substrate is the same argument. The substrate is the position.

John Mearsheimer wrote "The Tragedy of Great Power Politics" in 2001, and the argument is the argument that the great powers do what the great powers can do. The argument is not morality. The argument is capability. The substrate is the same argument. The substrate is the position that says the answer to the capability is the counter-capability. The substrate is the counter-capability that is in the hands of the many.

The founders

Steve Jobs gave the Stanford commencement address in 2005, and the standard is not the standard of the inventor. The standard is the standard of the craftsperson. The work is the work, and the work is the craft, and the craft is the thing you do not compromise on. The substrate is the thing you do not compromise on. The substrate is the thing you ship, and the substrate is the thing you ship well.

Jesse Itzler wrote "Living with a SEAL" in 2015, and the book I come back to when I need to remember that the discipline is the discipline, and the discipline is the work, and the work is the work. The lesson is the commitment. The lesson is that the commitment is the thing that compounds.

Elon Musk is the first-principles person, and the lesson is not the company. The lesson is not the rocket. The lesson is the first-principles. The lesson is that you can reason from the substrate up. The substrate is the thing you reason from. The substrate is the thing you do not take on faith.

The modern operators

David Friedberg founded The Production Board, which is exactly the position I argue for in the substrate line. The production layer is the food. The production layer is the energy. The production layer is the atoms. The bits ride on top of the atoms. The substrate is the production layer. The substrate is the thing you do not skip.

Lex Fridman built a long-form interview format that is the format I want for the conversations I have, the conversations I write, the conversations I record. The format is the long form. The format is the patience. The substrate is the willingness to sit with the question for the time it takes to answer the question.

Chamath Palihapitiya founded Social Capital and co-hosts the All-In Podcast, and the argument is the argument that the asymmetric bet is the bet that wins. The bet is the bet if the upside is large and the downside is small. The substrate is the asymmetric bet.

The line

The line is the only thing that matters. The line is whether the AI is yours. Is it on your hardware. Does it use your memory. Is it pointed at your intentions. Is it yours, in the same way your house is yours, in the same way your tools are yours, in the same way your time is yours.

If a piece of writing keeps the line, the writing is right. If it loses the line, the writing is wrong. The line is the test. The line is the only test.

I have a hand on the wheel. I am not a passenger in this. I am a participant. I build the substrate. I build the thing that is mine. I build the thing that is yours if you want it. I build the thing that is on your hardware, that uses your memory, that is pointed at your intentions. I build the line.

I would rather have a hand in it than watch it happen to me. If you are building something that puts capable, aligned intelligence in more people's hands, I would love to hear from you. If you are building something that puts it in fewer, I would love to hear from you anyway, because the conversation is the only way any of this gets better.
