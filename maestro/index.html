The problem it kills

A rented router that optimizes their margin, not yours

A gateway you rent decides which model you get, on whose hardware, at whose price — and there is no second seat to fall to when it caps. The intelligence lives somewhere else, on someone else's roadmap.

One vendor owns your intelligence

One API, one bill, one roadmap. When they cap, neuter, or reprice a model, your whole stack inherits the decision — and there's no second seat to fall to. The routing lives on their hardware, not yours.

A cap stops everything

On a rented endpoint, a 429 or a daily cap is a hard wall — the work just halts until the window resets. One exhausted seat and your agents are stuck, with nowhere automatic to route to.

How it works

A routing brain you run across your fleet

A single OpenAI-compatible endpoint on your hardware, a learned router behind it, and a durable decision you can audit. Local first; frontier only when the task earns it — and a seat chain always ready underneath.

02

The routing path

One request in. The right model out — with a seat chain underneath.

A prompt enters the OpenAI-compatible endpoint, Vega scores it across seven tiers, and the call lands on the best model that clears the bar — local first. If that seat is exhausted, the failover engine advances to the next; every route writes a durable decision.

  • Local-first. On-device models handle what they can; nothing leaves unless a frontier model truly earns the call.
  • A cap is a non-event. An exhausted seat advances down the chain automatically — the redaction gate still runs before any frontier egress.
  • Durable by default. Each decision is fetchable at /v1/receipt/{id} with the model, tier, and cost.
