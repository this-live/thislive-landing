Why local

The rented stack optimizes the provider's margin. Not yours.

Every mainstream approach forces a trade between capability and ownership. Cortex refuses the trade — each problem maps to the pillar that ends it.

The problem

Provider lock-in & runaway token cost

You rent intelligence by the token. Persistent agents become an open-ended tax, and switching providers means rebuilding everything around their API.

The pillar

Maestro routes by task, cost & privacy

Send each call to the right model — local first, frontier only when it earns it. BYOK or fully local, so cost falls as more work moves onto your own hardware.

Maestro →

The problem

Context windows are fake memory

A 200K window is not 200K of memory. Models degrade at the tail of long contexts, every session resets, and you pay to re-process your own data again and again.

The pillar

Mnemos remembers locally, scoped & labeled

One memory plane every agent shares — project-scoped by default, write-protected at the root. Memory compounds on your hardware instead of being re-bought each session.

Mnemos →

The problem

Surface sprawl & duplicated work

One agent for writing, one for coding, one for research — six subscriptions, no coordination. Every agent re-discovers what the others already knew, and you pay twice.

The pillars

Surfaces & Agent Fabric unify the work

Three front doors over one shared backend, and a deterministic execution spine that runs all your agents in parallel with durable receipts — shared context, no double spend.

Surfaces & Fabric →

The problem

You depend on someone else's model

When a provider neuters, deprecates, or reprices a model, your stack inherits the decision. You can't specialize it on your own work, and you can't take it with you.

The pillar

Forge turns your work into your models

A gated adapter factory that can't ship a claim it can't prove. The base model serves on your own GPU now; the factory is built — the first measured local adapter is the milestone.
